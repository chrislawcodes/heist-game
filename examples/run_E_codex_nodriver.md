# Heist Report: The Penthouse Caper

## Strategy

> I want to do this without a Driver. We will walk away. The crew should be designed around small targets that don't require a getaway vehicle — places we can leave through the front door, walking calmly, in a way that doesn't draw attention. Pick a low-key job; we are not breaking out of an armored car standoff. Bias toward Penthouse-style or quiet residential targets. Maximum four crew members, all of whom can pass as civilians. No Muscle specialists — if we need violence, we've already failed. Risk tolerance: low. Decline every bonus pursuit; we are not greedy. If complications surface, I want the crew to back off and try again another night. This is about proving we can do this without the loud, fast escape.

## Casting

I spent **$1,300 of the $2,000 bankroll** and kept the crew to four people who can plausibly pass as civilians: **Rook Ferreira**, **Sasha Kuznetsova**, **Theo Ashland**, and **Eli “Owl” Park**. Rook is the expensive anchor at $700 because he gives us **High Physical** coverage by himself, which matters if a quiet residential target still has a serious safe. Sasha and Theo are both bargain Medium specialists at $200 each, and Eli is the glue pick: his Low hacker and Low inside-man skills pair upward with theirs, giving us stronger coverage without paying for another premium specialist.

The main trade-off is that we have **no driver and no muscle**, by design. That rules out anything with serious armed response or a hot exit. It also means we are not built to improvise through violence if things go wrong. The upside is that the crew is cheap, small, low-profile, and shaped around exactly the kind of job the player asked for: one where the best escape is looking like we were never there.

I picked **The Penthouse Caper** because it is the only job on the slate that really respects the strategy. Its profile is **Electronic Medium, Physical Medium, Confrontation None, Social Low**, with no Hard challenges to create a trap. Rook handles the safe work, Sasha and Eli handle the systems, Theo and Eli cover the civilian-facing moments, and if the night starts turning loud, we back off instead of chasing a bonus or forcing the exit.

## Heist

### Scene 1 — Setup & approach

_**Personnel:** Theo Ashland, Eli "Owl" Park · **Reasoning:** Setup and approach is a Social scene: Theo leads as the Medium inside-man, reading the building rhythm, staff habits, and resident traffic, while Eli supports in the same area. Paired together, they operate above Theo's solo level, keeping the approach calm and civilian without exposing Sasha or Rook before their work is needed._

Theo Ashland arrived first, not early enough to look eager and not late enough to be remembered. Gray overcoat, soft leather folio, the face of a man who belonged in lobbies with polished stone and flowers changed twice a week.

He paused at the curb outside the penthouse building and looked up only once.

“Never stare at the money,” he murmured into the pin mic. “Money gets stared at all day. Staff notice envy faster than they notice crime.”

Across the street, Eli “Owl” Park sat on a bench with a paper coffee cup and a messenger bag full of nothing useful except the shape of usefulness. He watched reflections in the glass doors instead of the doors themselves.

“Doormen rotate every forty minutes,” Eli said. “Left desk guard checks packages, right desk guard checks faces. Elevator attendant smiles at residents, not guests. That means guests are normal tonight.”

Theo smiled faintly. “Good. We love normal.”

A catering van pulled away from the service entrance. Two residents came out arguing about ski flights. A dog walker slipped in with three leashes and no one asked her a question.

Theo crossed with the light, entered with the small confidence of a man expected somewhere upstairs, and gave the lobby a courteous nod instead of a performance. Eli followed thirty seconds later, dampening his pace to match the building: no hurry, no fear, no mission.

The lobby smelled like lilies and old money. The doorman glanced up.

Theo did not reach for an ID. He let the man ask first.

“Evening, sir.”

“Evening,” Theo said, warm but forgettable. “I’m here to confirm tomorrow’s installation window for 41B. I’ll wait by the desk if needed.”

The trick was not the lie. The trick was offering to be delayed.

The doorman relaxed by half an inch.

Behind him, Eli drifted toward the directory wall, eyes soft, shoulders loose, already mapping cameras, blind spots, and the elevator pattern.

In Theo’s ear, Sasha whispered, “You two look painfully respectable.”

Rook added, dry as dust, “Try not to enjoy it.”

Theo touched two fingers to his cufflink and smiled at no one.

“Approach is clean,” he said. “We leave the same way.”

### Scene 2 — Challenge — social

_**Personnel:** Theo Ashland, Eli "Owl" Park · **Reasoning:** This is a Medium Social challenge, so Theo should lead with his Medium inside_man skill. Eli supports in the same area with Low inside_man, which lifts the pair above Theo's solo level and gives us a cleaner margin against the cleaning-service complication without escalating or exposing the technical crew. · **Mechanical outcome:** success_

The elevator opened on forty-one with a soft chime and the smell of lemon polish.

Theo did not move first. He let the doors part, let the hallway reveal itself, let the problem announce its own shape.

A cleaning cart stood outside 41B.

Eli’s voice came low through the comms. “That cart was not on the lobby schedule.”

“No,” Theo said, stepping out as if this were mildly inconvenient instead of dangerous. “It was not.”

Halfway down the hall, a woman in a navy cleaning-service polo backed out of the penthouse door with a vacuum cord in one hand. She saw Theo, then Eli, then Theo’s folio.

Theo gave her the tired smile of a contractor trapped between departments.

“Please tell me I’m not the second person today sent up here without anyone notifying you.”

The woman stopped. Suspicion did not vanish, but it changed clothes. Now it looked like workplace irritation.

“What company?” she asked.

“Marwick Residential Systems,” Theo said. “Tomorrow’s glass calibration. I’m just confirming sight lines before the installers bring equipment through and block your whole corridor.”

Eli stepped in, soft and apologetic. “If we’re in your way, we can wait. Last thing we want is to make you redo floors.”

That landed. People who had never cleaned a floor rarely thought about making someone redo it.

The cleaner looked from Eli to Theo, then back toward the open apartment. “You need inside?”

Theo shook his head immediately. Too fast would be eager. Too slow would be a lie forming.

“Door frame and elevator approach only. Three minutes. You keep your room, we keep ours.”

She studied them a second longer, then pointed two fingers at the hallway ceiling. “Don’t touch the walls. They complain about everything.”

Theo gave a small, grateful nod. “That sounds exactly like them.”

Eli crouched near the baseboard as if checking measurements, using the angle to see the latch plate, the service closet, and the camera dome tucked beside a sprinkler head.

In his ear, Sasha whispered, “You’re clear. She bought boring.”

Rook’s voice followed. “Boring is underrated.”

Theo opened his folio and made a meaningless note.

“Mid-shift confirmed,” he murmured. “No panic. We work around her, not through her.”

### Scene 3 — Challenge — electronic

_**Personnel:** Sasha Kuznetsova, Eli "Owl" Park · **Reasoning:** This is a Medium Electronic challenge, so Sasha leads with her Medium hacker skill. Eli supports in the same hacker area, lifting the pair above Sasha's solo level and giving the crew a quiet margin while Theo and Rook stay unexposed for their own parts. · **Mechanical outcome:** success_

Sasha did not come upstairs.

That was the point.

She sat two blocks away in the back corner of an all-night tea shop, laptop open, scarf loose around her neck, looking like a graduate student punishing herself with bad lighting. Her screen showed a tidy little map of the penthouse building’s resident network, the kind sold as luxury because it turned every door, camera, thermostat, and leak sensor into one expensive nervous system.

“Eli,” she said, “camera beside the sprinkler head is on a local repeater. I need the service panel near you awake but not opened.”

In the hallway, Eli shifted his messenger bag against the wall and let his shoulder brush the panel trim.

“Define awake.”

“Convince it someone important is nearby.”

“That is a strange thing to say to a box.”

Theo, still pretending to review installation notes, murmured, “You’ve said stranger things to people.”

Eli pulled a slim fob from his sleeve and palmed it near the panel. One pulse. Then another. Nothing visible changed, but in Sasha’s headphones the network coughed up a new handshake.

“There,” Sasha said. “Good little box.”

Rook’s voice came through, flat and patient. “Do not flirt with infrastructure.”

“I respect infrastructure,” Sasha replied. Her fingers moved without drama. “That is why it disappoints me when rich men install consumer-grade access control and call it bespoke.”

A live camera feed blinked on her screen: hallway, elevator, cleaning cart, Theo’s neat gray shoulders, Eli pretending to measure nothing.

She did not kill the camera. Dead cameras drew phone calls. Instead she gave it a memory. The hallway would loop itself in thirty-second intervals, with enough variance in shadow and timestamp jitter to look bored rather than frozen.

“Camera is dreaming,” Sasha said. “Door contact will report closed for six minutes after opening. Motion inside 41B stays local unless someone manually checks the app.”

Eli glanced toward the cleaner humming somewhere inside. “And if she walks past the camera?”

“She already did,” Sasha said. “Twice, according to the system. Very productive woman.”

Theo closed his folio.

“Clean lane?”

“For now,” Sasha said. “And only for now. Do not fall in love with my miracles.”

Rook exhaled once.

“Six minutes is not a miracle. It is a budget.”

### Scene 4 — Challenge — physical

_**Personnel:** Rook Ferreira · **Reasoning:** This is a Medium Physical challenge, and Rook's High safecracker skill clears it alone with margin. No second crew member has safecracker skill, so adding anyone would only increase exposure without improving the mechanical coverage. · **Mechanical outcome:** success_

Rook entered 41B like a man visiting a room he had already decided to forgive.

No wasted glance at the skyline. No admiration for the white marble, the black steel staircase, the art placed exactly where guests would know it was expensive. He crossed the penthouse on soft soles while Sasha’s borrowed camera dream repeated an empty hall outside.

“Safe is in the study,” Eli whispered. “North wall. Behind the walnut panel.”

Rook stopped before the panel and ran two fingers along the grain.

“Not walnut,” he said.

Theo, from the hallway, almost laughed. “That feels secondary.”

“It is never secondary.”

Rook found the pressure catch under a shelf of unread architecture books. The panel sighed inward, revealing a compact wall safe with a digital keypad and a mechanical override hidden beneath a sliding plate.

Sasha’s voice sharpened. “You have four minutes, twenty.”

“I need less than three.”

“That confidence or schedule?”

“Both, if people stop asking.”

He did not attack the safe. That was amateur theater. He listened first, one hand resting near the hinge, the other coaxing the override with tools so thin they looked drawn rather than made. A soft click. Then another, deeper and more honest.

In the hallway, the cleaner’s vacuum cut off.

Everyone went still.

Theo spoke first, easy and warm, voice angled away from panic. “All good out here. She’s changing outlets.”

Rook’s eyes did not move from the dial.

“Then let her.”

The safe resisted at the final gate, just enough to show pride. Rook adjusted by touch, not force. His mouth tightened.

“This is not a vault,” he murmured. “It is a rich man’s promise to himself.”

The lock gave.

Inside: velvet trays, document sleeves, two wrapped bundles, and a slim case marked with a private jeweler’s seal. Rook took only what matched the job notes. No bonus hunting. No curious pockets.

Sasha counted down. “Two minutes, ten.”

Rook closed the safe, reset the panel, and wiped the edge where his glove had rested.

“Done,” he said.

Theo answered from the hall. “Then we remain boring.”

Rook slipped the case into a flat gray courier pouch.

“Boring,” he said, “pays better than brave.”

### Scene 5 — Transition to exit

_**Personnel:** Theo Ashland, Eli "Owl" Park · **Reasoning:** The exit transition is about staying ordinary, timing movement through shared space, and avoiding attention. Theo leads the civilian-facing movement with Medium inside_man skill, while Eli supports in the same area to strengthen the social cover. Rook carries the take quietly, and Sasha remains off-site to keep the electronic window clean until they are out._

Theo checked his watch, though the time that mattered was in Sasha’s voice.

“Camera loop expires in fifty seconds,” she said. “After that, the hallway remembers how to see.”

Eli stood from his crouch near the baseboard, rolling one shoulder like a tired technician. “I hate when buildings regain consciousness.”

Rook came out of 41B with the gray courier pouch flat under his arm, no bulge, no hurry, no victory on his face. He looked less like a thief than a man disappointed by someone else’s maintenance standards.

Theo closed his folio and gave the cleaner a polite half-wave through the open door.

“Thank you for working around us,” he said. “If anyone complains tomorrow, please blame the glass people. We are used to it.”

She snorted. “Everyone blames the glass people.”

“As they should.”

That was the exit: not silence, but a clean little thread of normal conversation, tied off without a knot.

They moved toward the elevator in a loose line. Theo first, then Eli, then Rook with the pouch, spaced like coworkers who did not particularly like each other but had survived a site visit. No one looked back at the penthouse door. Looking back was for people leaving something behind.

“Thirty seconds,” Sasha said.

The elevator took its time arriving.

Eli watched the numbers above the doors crawl upward from thirty-eight.

“That is rude,” he whispered.

Theo kept his eyes on his folio. “Elevators are allowed to be rude. We are not.”

Rook shifted the pouch from left hand to right. “If the door opens full, we abort downward on stairs.”

“Agreed,” Theo said, without drama.

The doors opened empty.

They stepped in. Eli pressed Lobby with the bored firmness of a man who had pressed many lobby buttons and expected nothing meaningful from this one.

“Loop expired,” Sasha said.

The camera feed became honest again just in time to watch three respectable men inside an elevator, leaving a floor where they had already been seen, already explained, already forgotten.

Theo exhaled through his nose.

“Front door,” he said. “Same faces. Same pace.”

Rook looked at the polished elevator wall.

“No souvenirs,” he said.

Eli smiled faintly. “Except the one in the pouch.”

### Scene 6 — Escape

_**Personnel:** Theo Ashland, Eli "Owl" Park · **Reasoning:** With no driver by design, the escape depends on walking out as civilians rather than making a fast getaway. Theo leads the social cover with Medium inside_man skill, and Eli supports in the same area to strengthen the calm front-door exit while Rook stays quiet with the pouch and Sasha remains off-site as overwatch. · **Mechanical outcome:** success_

The lobby had changed by exactly one flower arrangement and no alarms.

That was how Theo liked it.

He stepped out of the elevator with the same mild fatigue he had carried in, folio tucked under one arm, expression tuned to professional inconvenience. Eli followed, eyes down for two seconds, up for one, never enough to look like he was checking exits. Rook came last with the courier pouch, his face unreadable, his pace ordinary.

The doorman looked over.

Theo gave him a small nod. “All set for tomorrow. Tell 41B the installers will want the service elevator cleared by nine.”

The doorman sighed like a man already tired of someone else’s renovation. “I’ll note it.”

“Appreciated.”

No one stopped them.

No one hurried them.

Outside, the night air felt colder than it had six minutes ago. Traffic slid by in wet ribbons of red and white. No engine waited at the curb. No driver. No doors flung open. No tires screaming them into every camera in midtown.

Just three men walking away from a building that still believed they had belonged there.

In their ears, Sasha said, “I have no building alert, no manual camera review, no concierge call upstairs. Heat is zero.”

Eli adjusted his scarf. “I would like that engraved somewhere.”

Rook turned left at the corner, not toward the tea shop, not toward a subway entrance yet, just into the normal pedestrian current. “Engraving is evidence.”

Theo smiled. “He means emotionally, Rook.”

“Worse.”

They split at the next block without ceremony. Theo crossed with a cluster of theatergoers. Eli ducked into a pharmacy and came out without the messenger bag. Rook kept walking until the pouch became a folded newspaper inside a different tote.

Two blocks away, Sasha closed her laptop and left exact change under her cup.

“No chase,” she said softly. “No panic. No bonus. We are done.”

Theo’s voice came back calm and pleased. “Then we did what we came to prove.”

Rook answered last.

“Walking away is still an escape.”

## Epilogue

They did not meet that night.

That was part of the discipline. No champagne, no back room, no clever little toast over a bag full of somebody else’s diamonds and documents. The take moved once, then slept. Four hundred fifty thousand dollars became a number in Theo’s head, a weight in Rook’s hands, a clean absence in Sasha’s logs, and a story Eli refused to tell anyone.

By morning, the penthouse owner blamed staff, then contractors, then himself for trusting a wall safe that looked better than it worked. The cleaning service got an angry call and gave better than it received. The building pulled camera footage and found nothing but normal people doing normal things.

Theo read that twice over coffee and allowed himself one quiet smile.

Sasha sent a single message to the crew: **No encore.**

Rook replied: **Good.**

Eli waited a full minute before adding: **But it was very elegant walking.**

No one disagreed.
