Sasha knelt by the cold steel face of the vault, the dial cool under her fingertips. The room smelled of dust and old paper. Somewhere far above her, a string quartet eased into the second movement of a Brahms.

"You're at thirty-eight," Mara murmured in her ear. "Curator just walked into the east hall. You've got maybe four minutes."

Sasha didn't answer. She didn't need to. The first wheel had already whispered into place; she could feel it, more than hear it. She turned the dial back, slower this time, her breath shallow and even.

A click. Then another. The third tumbler was always the one that gave people away — they got greedy, leaned in, and the dial caught. Sasha let it breathe. The room held its silence around her like an old, patient friend.

The handle gave under her palm with a small, dignified sigh. Inside, the coin glass case sat exactly where the schematic said it would: matte black, unremarkable, worth every dollar of the take.
